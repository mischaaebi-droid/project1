A controversial, little-known
politician has the most influence

Pascal Broulis has the greatest impact in Swiss parliament, according to an analysis of 4,000 parliamentary proposals and government inquiries.

Liberal member of the Council of States Pascal Broulis has made headlines in French-speaking Switzerland several times, including over trips with Swedish billionaire Frederik Paulsen and allegations that he gave preferential treatment to wealthy taxpayers. Outside the French-speaking part of the country, however, Broulis is little known.

Yet my analysis shows that Broulis has been the most influential member of the Swiss parliament during the current legislative term. To produce the ranking, I scraped, collected and analysed 4,100 official parliamentary initiatives submitted by Swiss lawmakers during the current legislature.

Broulis submitted 18 proposals. Eleven were approved by both chambers of parliament and forwarded to the federal government for implementation. No other member of the federal parliament has successfully passed as many proposals during this legislative term. His success rate of 60 percent is also the highest.

Surprisingly, the liberal finance politician has not achieved most of his successes in the fields of taxation and public finance. On two occasions, he successfully pushed initiatives aimed at improving the situation for people in the Gaza Strip. In another case, he secured parliamentary backing for a mandate requiring the federal government to develop a comprehensive strategy for Switzerland's long-distance rail network. He was also instrumental in ensuring that police officers will no longer face penalties if they trigger a speed camera while responding to an emergency with flashing blue lights.

Second place goes to SVP member of the Council of States Esther Friedli, with nine successful proposals. At the other end of the ranking are lawmakers who submit four times as many proposals as Broulis without achieving a single success. Among them is SVP National Council member Lorenzo Quadri. With 42 proposals, he is the most active parliamentarian in Bern. Yet not one of his proposals has secured a parliamentary majority during the current legislature.

Besides motions and postulates, members of parliament can also submit written questions to the federal government, known as interpellations. Unlike motions and postulates, interpellations do not require parliamentary approval. The federal government is obliged to respond to every one of them. Their number therefore says little about political success and instead mainly reflects a lawmaker's level of activity. Once again, the exceptionally active Lorenzo Quadri tops the ranking.
